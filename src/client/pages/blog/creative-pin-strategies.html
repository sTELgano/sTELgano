Creative PIN Strategies

Unique PINs Without Memorising a Dozen Numbers

2026-04-29 8 min read

The Multi-Channel Problem

One channel is simple: choose a PIN, keep it in your head. Two or more channels introduce a genuine tension. You have three options, and none of them is obviously right:

  • Same PIN everywhere. Easy to remember. But a single PIN leak exposes every channel simultaneously — someone who sees you type your PIN, or learns it through any means, can now try it on every steg number they know you use.
  • Fully independent PINs. Maximum security. But memorising N truly random PINs scales badly. Five channels means five secrets, each of which is as important as the others.
  • A derivation rule. You memorise one master secret and one rule. The rule produces a unique PIN per channel from the steg number. Leaking one channel’s PIN reveals nothing about any other channel’s PIN. This article is about building your own rule.

The strategies below all follow the same structure: one master PIN (something you already know), combined with a rule applied to the steg number (something the system provides).

What the Protocol Covers and What It Doesn’t

The access credential is computed as:

SHA-256(phone + ":" + PIN + ":" + ACCESS_SALT)

Because the steg number is the leading input, a computed credential is bound to a specific channel. Replaying the credential hash itself on a different channel will fail.

But that is not how password managers work. A PM operates on raw form fields before any JavaScript runs. It prompts to save the digit string you typed, stores it, and auto-fills that same digit string later. The PM never sees the hash — only the raw PIN. If it auto-fills "1234" and you accept, the client reads your steg number from the phone field, computes the hash, and submits it. Whether that succeeds depends entirely on whether "1234" is the correct PIN for that channel — nothing in the hash prevents the PM from suggesting it.

sTELgano addresses this at the field level. The PIN field uses type="text" rather than type="password" — removing the primary signal browsers use to identify a credential pair. Password managers look for a type="password" field to identify the password and treat the preceding text field as the username; without it, they do not recognise the form as a login form. Visual masking is applied via CSS (-webkit-text-security: disc) so the field still shows dots. An autocomplete="one-time-code" marker adds a secondary hint. A derivation rule is still the recommended practice — it is the only measure that also covers raw PIN exposure (observation, disclosure), which no field attribute can prevent.

Credential Replay

An attacker intercepts the computed hex credential for channel A and submits it directly to channel B. The hash fails — different steg number, different expected value.

Covered by the protocol

Raw PIN Exposure

Someone learns the raw digit string you typed — by watching, shoulder-surfing, or any other means. If you reuse the same PIN everywhere, every channel is now accessible. PM saving is partially mitigated at the field level (see above), but observation and disclosure cannot be addressed technically.

Partially mitigated technically — derivation rule closes the gap

A derivation rule is the complete defence. The field-level measure reduces the chance a PM saves a PIN at all. But if it does save, and if someone observes your PIN, a derivation rule means channel A’s PIN (83012671) tells an attacker nothing about channel B’s PIN (83010842). The strategies below are how you build that rule.

How to Read the Examples

All examples below use the same two steg numbers so you can compare outputs across strategies:

Steg number A

+1 (415) 555-2671

Digits only: 14155552671

Steg number B

+44 7700 900 842

Digits only: 447700900842

Master PIN throughout all examples: 8301

Every strategy below is purely mental arithmetic — no writing, no device, no storage. The rule should be simple enough that you can reconstruct the PIN in your head at any time.

Strategy 1 — Suffix

Rule: master PIN + last N digits of the steg number.

Channel A (last 4):83012671
Channel B (last 4):83010842

The last four digits of a phone number (the subscriber number in North America, the line number in most numbering plans) are the most unique part of a local number. They are also the part most likely to differ between two steg numbers in the same country.

Caveat: Two steg numbers that differ only in a predictable way (e.g. sequential numbers) produce related PINs. Use if your steg numbers are randomly generated — as they are by the in-app generator.

Strategy 2 — Prefix

Rule: first N digits of the steg number + master PIN.

Channel A (first 4):14158301
Channel B (first 4):44778301

Using the first digits means you are anchoring on the country code and area code. Two steg numbers in the same country with the same area code will share the same prefix — the channel distinction is then carried entirely by the master PIN position. Prefer Suffix for stronger inter-channel separation if your steg numbers share an area code.

Strategy 3 — Reversed Suffix

Rule: master PIN + last N digits of the steg number, reversed.

Channel A (last 4 reversed):83011762
Channel B (last 4 reversed):83012480

Reversing the suffix adds a small step that makes the derivation less obvious to a casual observer who knows both your master PIN and the steg number. It is not a cryptographic defence — anyone who knows your rule can still compute the PIN — but it reduces exposure from pure memorisation leakage.

Strategy 4 — Middle Extraction

Rule: master PIN + digits at a fixed middle position (e.g. positions 4–7 counting from 1).

Channel A:14155552671 → 83015552
Channel B:447700900842 → 83010090

The middle of a phone number (the exchange prefix in North America, digits 5–8 in many European formats) varies enough to distinguish most randomly-generated steg numbers. Choose a position window that stays within the number length for all steg numbers you use.

Strategy 5 — Interleaving

Rule: alternate digits from master PIN and from the steg number (e.g. M1P1M2P2…).

Master: 8301 • Last 4 of A: 2671

Channel A:82360711

Master: 8301 • Last 4 of B: 0842

Channel B:80380412

Interleaving produces an 8-digit PIN from a 4-digit master and 4 steg digits. The resulting PIN carries both signals inseparably — knowing the PIN but not the rule reveals nothing about either component. Harder to reconstruct under pressure; write it down in a private test session first.

Strategy 6 — Digit Sum

Rule: master PIN + (sum of all digits of the steg number) mod 100, zero-padded to 2 digits.

Channel A: 1+4+1+5+5+5+5+2+6+7+1 = 42 → 42 mod 100 = 42

830142

Channel B: 4+4+7+7+0+0+9+0+0+8+4+2 = 45 → 45 mod 100 = 45

830145

The digit sum condenses the entire steg number into two digits. It is easy to compute in your head and fully reconstructable without writing anything down. The trade-off is that many phone numbers share the same digit sum — the space of possible suffixes is only 0–99. Use a larger modulus (1000, four digits) if you want stronger inter-channel differentiation.

Strategy 7 — Area Code Anchor

Rule: area code (or country code + first N local digits) as prefix, master PIN as suffix.

Channel A (area 415):4158301
Channel B (country+prefix 447):4478301

The area code is the geographic identity of a steg number. It is easy to identify by scanning the number and shifts naturally from country to country. If two of your channels happen to share an area code, use the full 6-digit prefix (country code + area code) as the differentiator.

Strategy 8 — Every Other Digit

Rule: take every even-indexed digit (positions 2, 4, 6, 8…) from the steg number, use the first 4, append to master PIN.

Channel A: 14155552671 → 4552

83014552

Channel B: 447700900842 → 4700

83014700

Sampling alternating digits spreads extraction across the whole number rather than concentrating it at one end. It is slightly harder to compute mentally — count along the digits and pick every second one. Practice the rule on your specific steg numbers before committing to it.

Strategy 9 — Nth Channel Index

Rule: for channel N (1, 2, 3…), take digits at positions N through N+3 in the steg number, append to master PIN.

Channel 1 (positions 1–4): 1415 5552671

83011415

Channel 2 (positions 2–5): 14155552671

83014155

This strategy is less about making different channels on the same steg number unique (you would need a different steg number per channel anyway) and more about having a consistent mental habit: “this is my second channel, so I start reading the steg number from position 2.” The rule reduces cognitive load across many channels because you never have to remember which suffix you chose — only your channel number.

Strategy 10 — Custom Position Rule

Rule: choose any four positions once, memorise them. For every channel, read the steg number digit at each of those positions.

Positions: 2, 5, 9, 11

Channel A: 14155552671 → 4561

83014561

Channel B: 447700900842 → 4004

83014004

You choose the positions once and keep them constant. The positions themselves become part of your private rule — they are as much a secret as your master PIN. Someone who sees your steg number does not know which positions you read. Someone who knows your positions still needs the master PIN. The two components are independent.

Honest Caveats

These strategies are practical tools, not cryptographic guarantees. A few things to keep in mind before adopting one:

  • Entropy is lower than independent PINs.

    A derivation rule means the PINs across your channels are correlated. If an attacker knows your master PIN and your rule, they can compute all your channel PINs immediately. Truly independent PINs are harder to crack even with partial knowledge. The trade-off is real: memorisation ease costs some theoretical entropy.

  • Two-channel compromise may reveal the rule.

    If an attacker learns both the steg number and the final PIN for two of your channels, they may be able to infer your derivation rule. This is consistent with sTELgano’s stated threat model (intimate-access attackers, not state surveillance) but is worth naming explicitly.

  • Rule reuse outside sTELgano defeats the benefit.

    If you apply the same derivation rule to produce PINs for unrelated services, a compromise elsewhere can leak information about your sTELgano channels. Treat the rule and the master PIN as a pair that only appears inside sTELgano.

  • Any rule is better than no rule.

    The primary alternative — reusing a single PIN across channels without any derivation — means a single observation gives an attacker access to every channel. Even a simple suffix rule breaks that link. Perfect is the enemy of good here.

Choosing Your Rule

The right rule is the one you will actually use reliably. A sophisticated rule you cannot reconstruct under pressure is worse than a simple rule you can apply in two seconds. Criteria, in order of importance:

  1. 01You can reconstruct it cold. Test your rule when you have not thought about it for a week. If you hesitate, simplify it.
  2. 02It produces visibly different PINs for your actual steg numbers. Apply the rule to all your steg numbers and check the results differ. Two steg numbers from the same country with the same area code may produce similar outputs under some strategies.
  3. 03It does not require a device or storage. A rule that needs a calculator or a note defeats the passcode test. Mental arithmetic only.
  4. 04The master PIN is genuinely private. The derived PINs are only as strong as the component you keep secret. A master PIN that is your birth year, your device PIN, or any other guessable number reduces the security of all channels simultaneously.

Pick one strategy, test it on your steg numbers, and commit to it. The goal is a habit that runs automatically, not a puzzle you have to solve each time you open the app.
